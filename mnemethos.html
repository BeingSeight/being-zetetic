μνήμη

The Greek word for memory. The root of the English mnemonic, amnesia, Mnemosyne — the mother of the Muses.

What Mnemethos is

Mnemethos is the name I gave the craft the protocol is built on. It is not a feature. It is a stance.

The Greek root μνήμη (mnēmē) means memory — but not memory as storage. Memory as the act of holding something in mind long enough to act on it. The Greek poets did not think of memory as a room you put things in. They thought of it as a discipline you practiced.

The suffix -θεός (-theos) means "of the divine" or "relating to the gods" — used in compounds like entheos ("full of the god") and theios ("of or from the divine"). Mnemethos, then, is something like: the making-divine of memory — the craft of treating what you hold in mind as worthy of care.

Why the protocol exists

In the AI era, we consume faster than we grasp. We have surface knowledge of everything and deep confidence in nothing. The fundamentals — the things that would, if we owned them, make us dangerous in the best sense — stay black boxes we never open.

Most learning tools optimize for throughput. They measure engagement, time-on-page, completion rate. They never measure retention in six months. They never ask whether you can rebuild the concept from a single seed question.

Mnemethos is the opposite stance. The protocol assumes that anything you don't earn — by being asked, by being forced to choose, by being made to defend — you don't own. The AI is a vehicle, not a source. The syllabus is the territory. The reader's mind is the only thing that counts.

How the protocol serves the stance

The Zetetic Thread forces the AI to never tell you what you could have asked. Every concept arises as a question from the previous one. Your mind is always one step behind the document — the document is always one question ahead of you. That's the discipline: don't let yourself be told what you can be led to ask.

The Zetetic Sift tests what survived. After you've read, the protocol returns with multiple-choice questions whose wrong answers are designed to be plausible. You don't just recognize the right answer — you confront the three almost-rights. The wrong answers are where real understanding hides.

The Zetetic-Socratic Hybrid alternates the two. Build, test, build, test. The document ends on a Sift — your final checkpoint is the proof of ownership.

The personal

I built this because I was tired of re-learning the same things every year. Tired of recognizing concepts I'd "studied" without being able to rebuild them from a question. Tired of summaries that felt like reading a Wikipedia article in the dark.

Mnemethos is the stance I wanted for myself. It's the stance I think the next era of learning needs — not more content, not faster tools, but a protocol that refuses to let you fake understanding.

If you use this protocol and it works — if a concept stays with you six months from now — then Mnemethos did its job. The memory is yours. The divine part is up to you.

Build something with it

Pick a mode, copy the prompt, paste it into a frontier model with a syllabus you care about. Don't skim. Don't ctrl-F. The protocol assumes you will read carefully — it will reward you if you do.
