Zetetic-Socratic Hybrid.

Thread + Sift, alternating phase by phase. Build a concept, test it, build the next. The complete protocol — for when you need to understand something forever, not just today.

Best for · complete mastery with retention
Format · thought-map interleaved with MCQs
Length · scales to syllabus depth

What it does

Hybrid interleaves the two modes. After every concept-building phase (Thread), a testing phase (Sift) confirms the concept actually landed. The reader doesn't just read — they're forced to choose, justify, and tether before moving on.

The Socratic element: the AI is forbidden from telling. It can only ask, and the reader's correct answer is the proof of understanding.

When to use it

  • The subject matters and you'll need it long-term, not just for an exam.
  • You want both the conceptual map and the test of recall in one document.
  • You're building a foundation you'll keep building on — fundamentals, first-principles domains.
  • You're tired of re-learning the same things every year.

The cycle

The document alternates ── THREAD ── and ── SIFT ── blocks. The cycle ends on a Sift block, so the final checkpoint tests the entire document.

— Phase 1 —

── THREAD ──

Open a concept with a question. Answer in one sentence. Raise the next question. 80–200 words.

— Phase 2 —

── SIFT ──

Test the concept with 2 MCQs. Each option justified in one line. Tether to what comes next.

— Phase 3 —

── THREAD ──

Build on what was just tested. The tether line in Sift sets up the next question.

— Phase N —

── SIFT ──

End on a Sift. Final checkpoint: 4 questions covering the entire document. No answers provided.

── THREAD ── ❓ What is a "system"? → A system is a set of parts that interact to produce a behavior that no single part produces alone. → The word comes from Greek systēma — "a whole composed of many". Remove one part, and the behavior changes — sometimes vanishes entirely. Think of a clock: gears, springs, hands. None shows the time. Together, they do. ➜ NEXT QUESTION: "If the pattern of interaction is what makes the system, what happens when the parts stop interacting — is the system gone?" ── SIFT ── 🧩 CONTEXT We just defined a system by its pattern of interaction. ❓ Q1: You take a clock apart and lay the gears on the table. The clock is now… A) Destroyed — the parts no longer work B) ✅ A collection — the parts are intact, the pattern is gone C) Still a clock — the system exists independent of arrangement D) A different system — the gears interact with the table ✅ Why B is right: A collection has the parts without the pattern. That's exactly what remains. ❌ Why A, C, D are wrong: (1 line each) 🔗 TETHER: This matters because in Phase 4 we'll see that some systems can be re-instantiated — the same pattern, different parts. 📍 CHECKPOINT 1. What makes a system a system? 2. Why is a pile of gears not a clock? (answers at the bottom of Phase 3)
