Zetetic Thread.

A pure thought-map flow. Every concept arises as a question from the previous one. The reader is never told anything they could have asked — they are led to ask it themselves, then given the answer in one clean sentence.

Best for · deep first-time understanding
Format · thought-map, plain text
Length · scales to syllabus depth

What it does

Zetetic Thread turns a syllabus into a sequence of questions, each arising naturally from the answer to the last. The reader's mind is never ahead of the document — the document is always one question ahead of the reader.

The word zetetic comes from the Greek zetein — "to seek, to inquire". The Thread mode is the purest expression of that: a single uninterrupted line of inquiry.

When to use it

  • You're learning something genuinely new and need a clean mental model from scratch.
  • The subject is dense and you don't trust a summary to capture it.
  • You want one pass through, not a textbook.
  • You're teaching yourself, not cramming for a test.

The structure

Each block is a single phase, ending with a next question — the question this answer naturally raises. Phases are short (80–200 words). One term per sentence. One sentence per term.

─── EXAMPLE · PHASE 1 ─── ❓ What is a "system", in the most general sense? → A system is a set of parts that interact to produce a behavior that no single part produces alone. → The word comes from the Greek systēma — "a whole composed of many". The implication: if you remove one part, the behavior changes. Maybe vanishes. Think of a clock as a system: gears, springs, hands. None of them shows the time. Together, they do. ➜ NEXT QUESTION: "If a system is defined by its parts interacting, what happens when the parts stop interacting — does the system stop existing, or just stop behaving?" ─── EXAMPLE · PHASE 2 ─── ❓ What happens when the parts stop interacting? → The system doesn't vanish — it becomes a collection. A pile of gears on a table is no longer a clock. The parts are the same. The behavior is gone. → This is the crucial distinction: a system is not its parts. It is the pattern of interaction between them. ➜ NEXT QUESTION: "If the pattern is what matters, can a system exist in two places at once — with different parts, the same pattern?"
